Norwood: Then & Now

Norwood transformation: A vibrant change in community landscape.

After the Civil War, this community emerged among African American soldiers from Indiana's only Black regiment, the 28th U.S. Colored Infantry, seeking to create a neighborhood that reflected their new liberty and dreams of a future. In 1872, these veterans became the first residents of what is known as Norwood. It is the oldest Black community in the U.S. that is still inhabited by descendants of the original settlers (IFF, 2024). Norwood is thought to be named after the Norwood family, who owned a farm in the area (Indianapolis Recorder, 2022). Norwood is a neighborhood located in southeastern Indianapolis. According to McDonough's article in the Indianapolis Recorder, Norwood runs from Prospect Street on the north, Sherman Drive on the east, Terrace Avenue on the south, and a line west of Vandeman Street (2022). Norwood consisted of thick woods and orchards when it was established, providing food and potential economic opportunities (Indianapolis Recorder, 2022). Norwood was created as a self-sufficient community, and the ownership of land represented independence and progress for African Americans during Reconstruction.
